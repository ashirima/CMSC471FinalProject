The Gap in Graduation Rates and its Impact on Occupation Employment

In many cases, higher paying professional careers are obtained by pursuing higher education. However, Black people generally have less access to higher education, which causes them to be underrepresented in higher paying fields. Below is a piechart that shows the breakdown of education attainment by race in adults aged 25 and older. Compared to their White and Asian counterparts, Black people are less likely to receive some sort of college degree.



With less Black Americans graduating in higher education, we then see a trend in the types of employment Black Americans are hired in. The following visual describes the under and overrepresentation of Black, White, Asian, and Hispanic Americans in four income levels. These income levels represent the jobs that have a weekly income under the first quartile (Low), between the first quartile and median income (Mid-Low), between the median the the third quartile (Mid-High), and above the third quartile (High).

From this visual we can see that Black Americans are statistically more likely to be overrepresented in lower paying jobs and underrepresented in higher paying jobs. This could be a result of the lack of education or the systemic impact of steering Black Americans to more service oriented jobs. (add source for data and this idea)
